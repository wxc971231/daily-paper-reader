Abstract —Large Language Models (LLMs) have advanced Automatic Heuristic Design (AHD) by enabling heuristic gen-eration through reasoning and code synthesis. Existing LLM-based AHD architectures mainly follow two paradigms: Natural Evolution, which uses crossover and mutation to explore heuristic programs, and Metacognitive Evolution, which refines reasoning through reflection. However, Natural Evolution discards reason-ing traces, weakening knowledge inheritance and exploitation, while Metacognitive Evolution lacks population-level recombina-tion, limiting exploration and increasing the risk of premature convergence. These limitations reduce search efficiency, stability, and solution quality on complex problems. To address this gap, we propose MeEvo, a dual-layer AHD framework that cyclically couples Natural Evolution and Metacognitive Evolu-tion. Natural Evolution explores heuristic code while recording reasoning traces, fitness values, and errors into a shared history; Metacognitive Evolution then reflects on this history to generate improved heuristics that re-enter the parent pool for the next cycle. This design enables population-driven exploration and reflection-driven refinement to reinforce each other. Experiments on five optimization problems with two LLM backbones show that MeEvo achieves stronger and more stable performance than existing LLM-based AHD architectures, especially on complex constrained tasks. 

Index Terms —Automatic Heuristic Design, Large Language Model, Natural Evolution, Crossover, Mutation, Metacognitive Evolution, Evolutionary Computation 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

# MANY real-world problems—from logistics and scheduling to network design—require optimization heuristics: rules of thumb that find good solutions efficiently [1]. Designing such heuristics demands deep expertise and is inherently problem-specific; the No Free Lunch theorem [2] further guarantees that no single heuristic works best for all problems, requiring repeated manual effort for each new task. Automatic Heuristic Design (AHD) [3] aims to automate this process, but classical approaches such as Genetic Programming [4] remain constrained by human-defined search spaces [5]. Large Language Models (LLMs) [6] have transformed AHD by combining evolutionary computation with neural code generation [7]. Existing approaches pursue two fun-damentally complementary strategies, distinguished by their primary evolution mechanism. Natural Evolution architec-tures (FunSearch [8], EoH [5], ReEvo [9], MCTS-AHD [10]) apply population-driven crossover and mutation to explore the code space broadly, generating diverse algorithmic strate-gies through recombination and variation. However, while some capture reasoning traces as byproducts (EoH) or em-ploy auxiliary reflection to guide search (ReEvo), none pre-serve reasoning as independently heritable information across generations—the strategic knowledge behind effective code is lost between cycles. Metacognitive Evolution (MeLA [11]) treats the LLM’s reasoning process itself as the evolution target through metacognitive reflection, systematically refining generative strategies based on performance feedback. This approach preserves and accumulates design knowledge, but operates as a single-layer local search: metacognitive reflec-tion refines existing reasoning patterns but cannot indepen-dently discover fundamentally novel algorithmic architectures through population-driven recombination. Prompt-level archi-tectures (OPRO [12], EvoPrompt [13], HiFo-Prompt [14], EvoPH [15]) evolve the instructions that guide code generation rather than the code or reasoning itself, and therefore address a different challenge from the two core paradigms. These two paradigms exhibit complementary deficien-cies . Natural Evolution architectures offer population-driven exploration—crossover recombines heterogeneous strategies, mutation introduces local innovations—but lack cognitive heritability: the reasoning traces that encode why design choices succeed or fail are discarded after each generation, so the population cannot accumulate strategic knowledge over time. Metacognitive Evolution offers reflection-driven cognitive heritability—it analyzes reasoning history to iden-tify effective patterns and avoid repeated errors—but lacks population-driven exploration: without crossover and mutation operating on a diverse parent pool, it cannot independently discover novel algorithmic architectures outside its current reasoning trajectory. Neither paradigm alone exploits the full potential of LLM-generated heuristics, which inherently com-prise two layers: the reasoning trace recording design rationale, and the executable code implementing algorithmic behavior. Recent hybrid architectures such as EoH-MR [16] and PyVRP + [17] have begun combining EC with metacogni-tive reflection. However, in both cases reflection remains an auxiliary module embedded within the code-generation loop rather than an independent evolutionary layer with dedicated operators. Neither provides a principled framework that cou-ples population-driven crossover and mutation with reflection-driven metacognition as equal, alternating partners—the fun-damental complementarity between code-level exploration and reasoning-level refinement thus remains unresolved.

A natural question is why MeEvo alternates rather than run-ning both layers in parallel. The two layers need incompatible conditions: Natural Evolution requires a stable parent pool for effective crossover, while Metacognitive Evolution requires accumulated reasoning history spanning many generations to distinguish real patterns from noise. Cyclic alternation resolves this tension in both directions. Natural Evolution runs for 

N generations to explore the code space and accumulate reasoning traces; Metacognitive Evolution then runs for M

generations, each analyzing the accumulated history to gen-erate improved heuristics that re-enter the parent pool for the next cycle. Alternation thus gives each layer the conditions it needs. This paper introduces MeEvo , a framework that combines Natural Evolution and Metacognitive Evolution through three contributions: 

(1) Systematic combination of Natural Evolution and Metacognitive Evolution. We couple population-driven crossover and mutation with reflection-driven metacognition in a unified AHD framework. Drawing on biology, where genotype carries heritable information and phenotype is the target of selection, reasoning traces (cognitive genotype) and heuristic code (algorithmic phenotype) are coupled through shared history: Natural Evolution records traces, Metacogni-tive Evolution reads them to produce refinements for the next cycle. 

(2) Cyclic alternation architecture with principled design rationale. We design a cyclic alternation architecture in which Natural Evolution and Metacognitive Evolution take turns rather than running in parallel. The two layers need incompat-ible conditions that parallel co-evolution cannot satisfy, and alternation resolves this tension by giving each layer its turn: 

N generations for exploration, M for reflection. 

(3) Intrinsic hierarchical exploration-exploitation bal-ance. We achieve hierarchical exploration-exploitation bal-ance intrinsically, without external scheduling. At the macro level, layer alternation provides structural balance between exploration and exploitation. At the meso level, a dynamic probability schedule within Natural Evolution shifts from crossover-dominated exploration to mutation-focused refine-ment as the population converges. We validate this framework through parameter sensitivity analysis, statistical significance testing, and experiments across five problems under two LLM backbones.

The consequence is a lack of cognitive heritability: population-driven exploration discovers diverse strategies but cannot accumulate design knowledge over evolutionary cycles. 

B. Metacognitive Evolution for AHD: Reflection-Driven Re-finement 

A complementary thread targets the LLM’s reasoning pro-cess itself through metacognitive reflection —the capacity to monitor, evaluate, and regulate one’s own cognitive processes [18]. This is distinct from reasoning enhancement techniques such as Chain-of-Thought (CoT) [19] and Tree-of-Thought 3

(ToT) [20], which structure how the model thinks but do not govern whether its current strategy is effective. Metacognition is a higher-order regulatory layer—the ability to step back and assess whether the current reasoning trajectory is productive [21]–[23]. MeLA [11] instantiated metacognition for AHD. Rather than evolving code or task descriptions, MeLA evolves the reasoning traces during heuristic generation by reflecting on previous outputs. However, MeLA operates as a single-layer system: reasoning and code are entangled within the same evolutionary loop. Metacognitive reflection refines existing reasoning traces but cannot independently discover fundamen-tally novel algorithmic architectures—it lacks the diversity that population-driven crossover and mutation provide. Emerging hybrid architectures represent early steps toward combining these two paradigms. EoH-MR [16] augments EoH with CMA-ES local optimization and a meta-cognitive reflection mechanism that monitors population diversity and stagnation to guide operator selection. PyVRP + [17] introduces a Reason-Act-Reflect cycle for metacognitive heuristic evolu-tion. However, metacognitive reflection remains an auxiliary module serving code generation rather than an independent evolutionary layer. In EoH-MR, meta-cognitive monitoring operates on the population as a guidance signal; in PyVRP +,the Reason-Act-Reflect cycle refines individual solutions but does not alternate between population-driven exploration and metacognitive reflection as independent evolutionary layers. Consequently, neither provides a principled mechanism that treats the reasoning process as an independently evolvable entity bidirectionally coupled with code-level evolution. The progression from Natural Evolution architectures to Metacognitive Evolution, with recent hybrid efforts beginning to bridge them (TABLE I), reveals a consistent pattern. Even in the most advanced architectures, population-driven crossover and mutation and reflection-driven metacognition have never been combined as equal, alternating partners in a unified framework. 

C. Biological Inspiration: Why Natural and Metacognitive Evolution Can Be Coupled 

The separation of reasoning traces from heuristic code is inspired by two principles from evolutionary biology. 

Genotype-phenotype distinction. In biology, the genotype carries heritable information and the phenotype is the observ-able organism shaped by environment; evolutionary operators act on the genotype, selection on the phenotype [24], [25]. We apply this distinction by treating reasoning traces as a cognitive genotype (carrying design knowledge) and heuristic code as its algorithmic phenotype (subject to fitness evaluation). 

Epigenetic regulation. Epigenetic regulation controls gene expression without altering DNA, responding to environmental signals [26]. We apply this analogy to metacognitive reflection: the reasoning history is an immutable record, and metacogni-tive reflection generates improvement signals from this history without modifying the traces themselves. These biological analogies serve as conceptual inspiration only; the contribution is the engineering combination of Nat-ural Evolution and Metacognitive Evolution. 

D. Exploration-Exploitation Mechanisms in AHD 

A central challenge in optimization is balancing exploration 

(searching broadly for new promising regions) against ex-ploitation (refining solutions in known good regions) [27], [28]. In classical algorithms, this balance is controlled by numerical parameters—crossover and mutation rates in GA [29], inertia weights in PSO [27], or temperature in SA—but the optimal setting varies by problem and changes during the search. LLM-based AHD introduces a new possibility: using the model’s reasoning capabilities for higher-level, semantic exploration-exploitation decisions rather than relying solely on low-level numerical parameters. Existing architectures adopt distinct strategies ( TABLE II ): HiFo-Prompt uses a Navigator rule engine; EvoPH uses policy sampling and island migration; EoH-MR uses meta-cognitive monitoring. All rely on external scheduling mechanisms. A critical distinction exists between MeEvo and parallel co-evolution architectures such as EvoPH. In parallel co-evolution, two populations evolve simultaneously and symmet-rically —each applies evolutionary operators independently, with information exchanged through explicit coupling mech-anisms (policy sampling, island migration). MeEvo differs in three fundamental ways. First, the layers alternate rather than run simultaneously, with N and M controlling time allocation. Natural Evolution explores the heuristic space with a consis-tent parent pool, then Metacognitive Evolution synthesizes the accumulated history into directed improvements without inter-ference from concurrent code-level changes. Second, the rela-tionship is asymmetric : Natural Evolution produces reasoning traces as byproducts of code generation, while Metacognitive Evolution consumes them to produce refinements—reflecting the distinct nature of the two paradigms (stochastic variation vs. deterministic reflection). Third, exploration-exploitation balance is architecturally intrinsic , emerging from cyclic al-ternation without external coordination mechanisms. 

E. Summary and Identified Research Gap 

The literature reveals three interrelated gaps that persist even in the most recent hybrid architectures. Gap 1—Natural Evolution and Metacognitive Evolution have never been systematically combined. EC-based architectures (FunSearch, EoH, ReEvo, MCTS-AHD) apply population-driven crossover and mutation to explore code space, but discard reasoning traces and thus lack cognitive heritability. MeLA applies metacognitive reflection to reason about design choices, but lacks population-driven diversity. Recent hybrid architectures (EoH-MR, PyVRP +) incorporate reflection as an auxiliary module, but do not treat Natural Evolution and Metacogni-tive Evolution as equal, alternating partners with dedicated operators for each paradigm. Gap 2—No architecture treats reasoning as an independently evolvable layer within a principled dual-layer framework. B. Why Cyclic Alternation? 

A natural question is why MeEvo alternates rather than running both layers in parallel, as EvoPH [15] does for prompts and code. The two layers need incompatible condi-tions. Natural Evolution applies crossover to recombine parent heuristics, requiring a stable parent pool; if Metacognitive Evo-lution injects new heuristics mid-cycle, the reference set shifts and crossover becomes unreliable. Metacognitive Evolution analyzes accumulated reasoning history and requires traces spanning many generations to distinguish real patterns from noise; a few recent traces cannot provide this. Cyclic alterna-tion resolves this tension: Natural Evolution first explores the code space with a fixed parent pool over N generations, then Metacognitive Evolution reflects on the accumulated history over M generations and produces improved heuristics that re-enter the parent pool for the next cycle. Alternation thus gives each layer the conditions it needs. The ablation study (§IV-E) confirms that (2 , 1) outperforms single-layer variants and alternative ratios.

D. Natural Evolution: Population-Driven Crossover and Mu-tation 

In the Natural Evolution layer, the LLM acts as an evo-lutionary operator, applying population-driven crossover and mutation directly to heuristic code ( Algorithm A1 ). This layer embodies the principle of natural evolution: crossover recombines heterogeneous parent strategies to explore novel algorithmic regions, while mutation introduces targeted local refinements. Reasoning traces are produced as natural byprod-ucts of every code generation step and recorded in the shared history for subsequent metacognitive analysis. Inspired by the genotype-phenotype distinction (§II-C), this layer operates on the code (the observable phenotype) through stochastic variation, while the recorded reasoning traces form the heri-table cognitive genotype. The entire population is replaced by offspring each generation. Each iteration executes three steps: 

1) Parent Selection: Infeasible heuristics ( f (h) = ∞) are excluded. The remaining feasible heuristics Hv are sorted by ascending fitness, and the top-K form the parent pool P

(default K=5 ; see Section IV for sensitivity analysis). If fewer than two feasible parents exist, the iteration is skipped. 

2) LLM-Mediated Crossover: With probability pc, two parents ha, h b ∈ P are sampled without replacement, and LLMC ROSSOVER prompts the LLM to: (1) analyze each par-ent’s distinct algorithmic approach, (2) identify complemen-tary strengths, (3) synthesize a hybrid offspring that combines components in novel ways, and (4) introduce at least one enhancement not present in either parent. By recombining heterogeneous strategies, crossover explores regions of the heuristic space inaccessible to single-parent variation. The LLM outputs a reasoning trace (the reasoning byproduct 

T h of f ) explaining component provenance and the rationale for novel combinations. 

3) LLM-Mediated Mutation: With probability pm = 1 −pc,a single parent is sampled, and LLMM UTATE prompts the LLM to refine the algorithm’s strategy through targeted local modifications. Problem-type-specific guidance is provided as illustrative examples: adaptive step-size tuning, Cauchy per-turbation, chaotic local search, among others, for continuous optimization; 2-opt/3-opt, swap, inversion operators, among others, for combinatorial optimization. The LLM is not re-stricted to these named techniques. Mutation must improve the heuristic through focused refinement of its search mechanisms, not merely adjust numerical parameters.

E. Metacognitive Evolution: Reflection-Driven Refinement 

Whereas Natural Evolution explores the code space through stochastic variation, Metacognitive Evolution refines the search through directed reflection on accumulated design knowledge. The LLM first analyzes the history of reasoning traces and their fitness outcomes, then translates these strategic reflections into improved heuristics. This layer embodies the 7

principle of metacognition: stepping back from individual code-generation steps to evaluate and regulate the overall search strategy. 

1) Two-Step Reflection-to-Code Pipeline: The process oc-curs in two steps. First, the metacognitive LLM distills the accumulated records—reasoning history T H , fitness history 

F , error records ERR , and current best h∗—into a Meta Insight: Meta = R(T H, F, ERR, h ∗) (3) where R is the metacognitive reflection function. The Meta Insight is a structured natural-language report containing three components: Convergence Diagnosis (classifying the search state as premature convergence, steady improvement, oscilla-tion, or stagnation), Limitation Analysis (identifying cognitive limitations and repeated ineffective strategies), and Pathway Recommendation (proposing specific optimization pathways with named algorithmic techniques). Second, the insight mapping LLM translates the Meta Insight into executable code with a reasoning trace: 

ht+1 = G(Meta , AP A ), T h t+1 = T (Meta ) (4) where G generates code and T generates the reasoning trace. Separating reflection from code generation into two steps is important: the Meta Insight captures what should change and 

why at a strategic level, while insight mapping determines how 

to implement these changes in code. This separation ensures that the reflection is grounded in accumulated history rather than being influenced by the immediate code generation task, and it allows the Meta Insight to serve as an interpretable, reusable signal across multiple code generation iterations. The insight mapping step prompts the LLM to preserve the best heuristic’s strongest components while redesigning weak areas identified in the Meta Insight, producing code grounded in accumulated strategic knowledge rather than generated from scratch. This process is analogous to epigenetic regulation (§II-C): the reasoning history is an immutable record, and metacognitive reflection generates improvement signals from it without modifying the traces themselves. 

2) Convergence-Aware Reflection and Evolutionary Oper-ators: The Meta Insight performs three tasks. Convergence Diagnosis classifies the search state (premature convergence, steady improvement, oscillation, or stagnation) by analyzing fitness trends in F and error patterns in ERR . Limita-tion Analysis identifies cognitive biases and repeated inef-fective strategies. Pathway Recommendation proposes specific improvement pathways with named algorithmic techniques. These three tasks instantiate directed variation at the genotype layer—analogous to epigenetic regulation—complementing the stochastic variation (crossover and mutation) of Natural Evolution at the phenotype layer. ERR , h∗) carries reasoning traces and fitness outcomes for-ward, providing heritability across cycles.

A. Challenges in Real-World Problems 

A critical question is whether LLM-based AHD gener-alizes beyond classical benchmarks. We include two real-world problems—ACS and WSN—to test this: they demand reasoning about domain-specific constraints beyond generic algorithmic templates, testing whether AHD architectures re-main effective when LLMs lack domain-specific knowledge. Existing architectures perform poorly here without structured problem analysis; incomplete problem descriptions yield near-zero runnable heuristics for architectures such as EoH and ReEvo (Appendix Fig. 6), making evolutionary search infea-sible. MeEvo’s dual-layer architecture produces consistently executable heuristics where other LLM-based architectures break down. 

B. Experimental Setup 

We evaluate MeEvo on five optimization problems across three categories: two classical benchmarks (TSP-Construct, TSP-ACO), one black-box benchmark (BPP-ACO), and two real-world problems (ACS, WSN), using two LLM backbones selected for their strong open-source performance, API avail-ability, and low inference cost ( TABLE VI ). C. Parameter Sensitivity Analysis 

We distinguish two categories of parameters. α, β, and 

K are optimization hyperparameters internal to the Natural Evolution layer; their sensitivity is examined below. The cycle counts N and M , by contrast, are architectural parameters 

that define the dual-layer loop itself—varying them changes the fundamental structure of the framework rather than tun-ing a fixed architecture. Their validation is deferred to the ablation study (§IV-E), where removing or rebalancing the layers directly tests the architectural design. We evaluate vari-ants on two representative problems—TSP-ACO (constrained combinatorial) and ACS (real-world, multi-constraint)—using DeepSeek-V4-Flash, with all other parameters held at their default values ( TABLE V ). Each configuration is repeated 5 times; we report the mean objective.

Regarding parent pool size (Panel B), TABLE VII reveals a U-shaped sensitivity. K=2 underperforms due to insufficient genetic material for meaningful crossover (TSP rises to 5.772, ACS to 591.83). K=5 achieves the optimal trade-off: it main-tains sufficient diversity for effective recombination without relaxing selection pressure. This is consistent with truncation selection theory in evolutionary computation, where moderate selection pressure balances exploration and exploitation. 

D. Results and Analysis 

TSP-Construct. TABLE VIII compares constructive heuristics across three problem sizes. POMO achieves near-optimal performance at N =50 (Gap 0.42%) through deep re-inforcement learning trained on thousands of N =50 instances, effectively specializing to this distribution. LLM-based AHD architectures cannot match this at N =50 because they operate zero-shot, generating heuristics from reasoning about problem structure rather than learning from instance-specific data. This gap reflects a fundamental trade-off: POMO trades generality for specialization, and its performance collapses at N =200 

(19.99%) where the trained policy fails to transfer. MeEvo maintains consistent scalability: with DeepSeek-V4-Flash, it achieves Gaps of 11.55%, 12.03%, and 13.83% at N =50 ,10 

100 , and 200 , ranking first among all LLM-based AHD archi-tectures at every size. At N =200 , MeEvo surpasses POMO (13.83% vs. 19.99%), demonstrating the generalization advan-tage of LLM-based heuristic design: the LLM reasons about problem structure rather than memorizing instance-specific patterns, so the generated heuristics transfer more reliably to larger instances. With MIMO-v2.5-Pro, MeEvo achieves the best Gap at N =50 (11.71%) and N =200 (13.79%). 

TSP-ACO, BPP-ACO, ACS, and WSN. TABLE IX 

presents results on four problems. MeEvo achieves the best mean performance across all problems with both LLM back-bones. On TSP-ACO and BPP-ACO, MeEvo yields the lowest objectives under both LLM backbones, with gaps to the next best architecture remaining below 1.68% (TSP-ACO) and 0.25% (BPP-ACO). On ACS and WSN, performance differences are dramatic— these real-world problems have complex, multi-modal con-straint landscapes that demand higher-level reasoning. Tra-ditional metaheuristics (GA, PSO, ACO, SCSO) largely fail under the limited iteration budget (50 for ACS, 100 for WSN), which is deliberately set to match the LLM-based protocol. The substantive finding on TSP-Construct is the scalability pattern—MeEvo degrades by only 2.3 percentage points from N =50 to N =200 , com-pared to 19.6 points for POMO—rather than marginal differ-ences at any single size. The convergence of significant p-values across diverse problems and both LLM backbones indicates that MeEvo’s advantage is systematic rather than random. V. C ONCLUSION 

We introduced MeEvo, a framework combining Natural Evolution and Metacognitive Evolution for automatic heuristic design. Natural Evolution explores the code space through population-driven crossover and mutation; Metacognitive Evo-lution reflects on accumulated reasoning history to generate improved heuristics that re-enter the parent pool for the next cycle. The two layers alternate rather than run in parallel, resolving the incompatible conditions each requires. Inspired by the genotype-phenotype distinction, we treat reasoning traces and heuristic code as separately evolvable entities, and the cyclic architecture intrinsically balances exploration and exploitation. Across five problems spanning classical benchmarks and real-world constrained tasks, MeEvo consistently outper-formed existing LLM-based AHD architectures under two independent LLM backbones, with the largest gains on ACS (p=0 .028 ) and WSN (p=0 .004 ). Ablation confirmed that cyclic alternation is essential, with N =2 and M =1 providing the optimal balance between exploration and reflection. Signif-icance testing at α=0 .05 validated that MeEvo’s improvements are systematic (7 of 8 comparisons). Three limitations warrant discussion. First, MeEvo relies on LLM API calls whose cost scales with population size and evaluation budget. Second, the framework currently targets 13 

single-objective optimization; extending it to multi-objective settings remains an open challenge. Third, the dynamic proba-bility scheduling uses a simple linear schedule; whether adap-tive scheduling could further improve performance warrants investigation.
