# Better Together, in the Right Order: Classical-then-LLM Optimization for SE 

Srinath Srinivasan 

NC State University ssrini27@ncsu.edu 

Tim Menzies 

NC State University timm@ieee.org 

Abstract —A growing body of work combines large language models (LLMs) with classical optimizers for software engineering (SE) configuration tasks. Often, the classical optimizer is in charge: it owns the search loop and calls the LLM only to assist in subroutines (e.g. to warm-start the first generation, propose a mutation, or stand in as a surrogate). We report that there is much value in the reverse approach: seeding an LLM with the results from a cheap classical learner. We call this method SNAP2. Applied to over 100 SE tasks, it is the single best of all methods studied, reaching the top tier on 85% of tasks, ahead of the same LLM run alone (75%) and ahead of every method in which the classical optimizer retains control. It is also less expensive: relative to the LLM-alone method, it uses roughly 30% fewer tokens and runs 1.4 × faster, since the classical setup performs the inexpensive work, and the LLM is invoked only to finish. We conclude that it is unwise to study classical learners or LLMs in isolation: there is much value in combining the two, and in the order that combination is applied. 

Index Terms —software engineering optimization, large language models, warm-start, active learning, multi-objective optimization, configuration tuning 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Misconfiguration is one of the most expensive recurring failures in software. In M YSQL, A PACHE , and H ADOOP , over 40% of failures trace to configuration errors [12], as do most cloud production outages [13]. A single bad setting is not a marginal cost: S TORM ’s worst configuration runs 480 × slower than its best [14]. The problem is growing. With LLMs and “vibe coding” it is easy to add “just a few more options”, and modern systems already ship with thousands that few developers understand [15]: in 15 years P OSTGRES options grew 300% and M YSQL 600% [16]. Most go untouched, so users fall back on sub-optimal defaults [15]. Twenty-five years of search-based software engineering have attacked this with stochastic search, Bayesian optimization, and active learning [17]–[20]. Lately the field has tried to move some, or all, of that work onto large language models [3], [21]–[26], but it cannot agree on whether the LLM helps at all. Reports range from limited advantage over conventional heuristics [27], to useful only on small problems [3], to useful only as a search subroutine [24], to too slow to be practical [28]. We argue this disagreement reflects an evidence base too thin to settle its own disputes. Studies here often report five datasets or fewer [3], where per-dataset variance swamps the effect sizes separating methods. Worse, papers reaching opposite verdicts

Our results overturn the pessimism reported above. Across opt2 work, the classical optimizer is in charge and the LLM only assists, and crucially, the LLM acts first : it seeds, suggests, or prunes, then a non-LLM algorithm drives the search to its answer. We find more value in the reverse approach: let a cheap classical learner do the inexpensive early work, then hand its results to the LLM to finish. This fills the middle cell of Fig. 1 (classical seeds, LLM optimizes), a pairing no prior method occupies. We call it SNAP 2, and it is the single best method we studied, reaching the top tier on 85% of tasks: ahead of the same LLM run alone ( 75% ) and ahead of every method in which the classical optimizer keeps control. It is also cheaper than the LLM alone (roughly 30% fewer tokens and 1.4 ×

faster ) since the classical step does the inexpensive work and the LLM is invoked only to finish. We also find that the cheap classical learner itself, EZR ,while statistically inferior to SNAP 2, still performs strongly and runs thousands of times faster at zero token cost. EZR 

alone may therefore be the right choice under tight resource constraints, such as edge computing or restricted API budgets. This paper makes four contributions: 1) SNAP 2, an optimizer that seeds an LLM with the output of a cheap classical learner, the reverse of the usual warm-start direction. 2) A large-scale evaluation across opt1 , opt2 , and opt3 on 105 SE optimization tasks (Section V). 3) Evidence that order matters : combining a classical learner and an LLM beats either alone, and the cheap-classical-first ordering of SNAP 2 beats the LLM-led ordering used in prior opt2 work. 4) Repeatable, refutable science : all code, prompts, and data are released under an open source license. Section II motivates the problem for an SE audience; Section III describes our algorithms; Section IV covers the MOOT datasets, metrics, statistics, and labeling budget; Section V answers the RQs; and Sections VI and VI-A discuss results and threats to validity. II. B ACKGROUND 

A. Is This an SE Problem? 

As shown in Table I, the methods of this paper support a wide range of common SE problems. All share one concern: “find the best value for this slot”. Formally, these are configuration tasks , and SE has many:  

> •

Effort estimation: options for faster, better delivery;  

> •

Defect prediction: options that best locate defects;  

> •

Performance tuning: compile options for more speed, least energy;  

> •

Product lines: the best options per product;  

> •

Project health: running a project to attract committers;  

> •

Cloud allocation: the .yaml that best tunes the cloud;  

> •

Test-suite selection: most bugs, least runtime;  

> •

Cross-domain: old-project options that help a new one;  

> •

Process modeling: steering a system-dynamics model to match the institutions and patterns that build the software. Hand-tuned solutions to these problems are error-prone. Hence, the failure costs of Section I are the direct consequence. 

B. Why These Problems Are Hard 

The difficulty is the size of the space a good answer hides in. X264’s eleven parameters take roughly 1,536 hours of compile-and-test [35]; M YSQL’s 460 binary options, treated as independent, yield 2460 combinations (the observable universe holds only about 280 stars) [15]; 7 Z’s fourteen compression parameters generate about a million configurations [2]. Spaces this large must be pruned with knowledge heuristics, and the three regimes of Section I differ only in where that knowledge comes from. 

1) opt1: only LLMs: An LLM is a knowledge heuristic with no algorithmic search around it: it draws on what it learned from terabytes of text, prompted to surface the part overlapping the task. OPRO [21] is the canonical case: it feeds the model the trajectory of tried points and asks for the next, with later chain-of-thought variants [36]. 

2) opt2: LLM plus classical: Rather than discard decades of algorithmic work, opt2 mixes the two. The cleanest case is the warm start: evolutionary optimizers need a generation zero, and better initial candidates yield better final results, so Senthilkumar et al. ask an LLM to propose it [3], [37]. 

3) opt3: no LLM: SMAC fits a random forest to the data seen so far and uses it to pick what to label next, searching widely while uncertain, then exploiting the region the ensemble agrees is best [20], [29]. TPE does the same with two Bayesian models [30]; multi-objective variants maximize expected hypervolume (qEHVI [31], [38]) or decompose one hard problem into many easy ones (MOEA/D [32]); DEHB adds Hyperband’s multi-fidelity budgeting [39]. RRP , used here and described below, is another no-LLM method. We do not re-run these, and that omission is deliberate. At FSE’26, Ganguly et al. benchmarked SMAC3, TPE, and DEHB against EZR on this same MOOT corpus and found EZR was strongest [28]. III. A LGORITHMS , THE DETAILS 

In SE optimization it is usually cheap to see the space of options but expensive to measure their effects . For example:  

> •

A test suite of a billion cases is easy to write and slow to run.  

> •

Every parameter in a makefile is trivial to list, but running them all to measure energy and runtime is often too slow to attempt.  

> •

Analyst skill and tooling clearly lower the cost of good software, yet pinning a number on that effect takes real work. So all our methods adopt one meta-procedure, sequential model-based optimization (SMO): given a table split into x

(independent) and y (dependent) columns, find rows with better 

y values while inspecting the y values of at most B rows. We call B the optimization budget . It can be surprisingly small when SMO uses active learning [40]. A passive learner builds its model from all the data. An active learner instead reflects on the model built so far to pick what to label next, so it can learn from very little data by chasing the most informative example and skipping the redundant ones. Every method here scores a candidate the same way. Each proposed configuration is projected onto its nearest measured row, by feature distance ρ, so every guess maps to a row with a known label. The methods differ only in which rows they spend the budget on. We compare seven of them across the three regimes (Table II). The candidate space of opt1,opt2,opt3 algorithms is far too large for one paper to enumerate. Accordingly, for each regime we take the published state of the art and add our own where the design space is empty. That coverage is deliberately uneven, because the literature is uneven:  

> •

Opt1 is nearly bare: the field mostly assumed a standalone LLM could not optimize [27], leaving OPRO [21] as the one landmark which we extend into SNAP . 

> •

Opt2 gives us two recent methods, BS _LLM [3] and SYNTH CORE [37].  

> •

opt3 gives us EZR , the learner Ganguly et al. recommend after a large FSE’26 evaluation [28]. Cohen advises checking complex methods against simple ones [41], so we add two no-LLM floors: RANDOM and the 2018 search-based method RRP [42]. Four of the seven algorithms are quickly described.   

> •RANDOM

is the simplest. It randomly samples, labels, and sorts B rows, then returns the best (by Eq. (2)).   

> •RRP

(random rare-pair) picks two far-apart rows then projects every row onto the line between them (using the cosine rule 1). It splits at the median and recurses on the half nearer the better pole. It labels two rows per split, so it needs O(log 2 n) labels.  

> •

SYNTH CORE [37] seeds by clustered ensemble prompting. DBSCAN [43] pre-clusters the labeled rows. Across M

independent sessions, each with a reset context and a fresh per-cluster sample, the model is asked to synthesize a row better than the best seen so far. There is no chain-of-thought, no debate, no cross-talk between sessions. The 

M outputs are sorted and the best is returned.  

> •BS

_LLM (“baseline LLM”) is an un-clustered version of SYNTH CORE . One prompt over all the data guesses B

configurations. It is the naive warm-start proposer that the clustered S YNTH CORE should beat. The other three methods carry the paper’s argument, so we detail them next.

C. SNAP 2 (the intersection): the classical learner seeds the LLM 

SNAP 2 was not designed up front. It fell out of the results of this paper. Once every method had run (Section V), one number was hard to ignore: EZR uses no LLM and runs three orders of magnitude faster than SNAP , yet it trailed SNAP by only a few top-tier points. To say that another way, a nearly free method was almost as good as our best, most expensive one. That gap suggested a trade: i.e. use a little of the cheap method to save time on the more expensive one. As shown at the bottom of Algorithm 2, SNAP 2 replaces 

SNAP ’s random warm start with the output of a short EZR 

run. EZR spends the first half of its labeling budget acquiring informative rows. The labeled trajectory then becomes gener-ation zero for the OPRO -style loop, which spends the second half ( WARM START mode v=2 in Algorithm 2). The total label budget stays at B and nothing else changes. IV. E XPERIMENTAL SETUP 

A. Datasets: MOOT 

This study used the MOOT data from Table I. As shown in that table, these tabular data sets have  

> •

3-1000 independent x attributes;  

> •

1 to 8 dependent y attributes to be minimized or maxi-mized;  

> •

93 to 100,000 rows. To the best of our knowledge, MOOT is the largest collection of real SE optimization tasks assembled to date. As shown by the references (right-hand-side column, Table I), MOOT’s data come from papers by numerous SE authors, presented in leading venues (ICSE, FSE, TSE, IST, EMSE, TOSEM, ASE). MOOT’s tasks span configuration, performance tuning, product lines, project health, defect prediction, testing, cost estimation, cross-domain generalization, and text mining. Compared to MOOT, other resources (e.g., SPLOT) were narrow and are now offline. Toolkits like Pygmo or Platypus offer only synthetic benchmarks. MOOT’s datasets come from published studies, real performance logs, cloud systems, and tuning tasks where bad configurations cost time, money, and credibility. Of MOOT’s 127 tasks we keep the 105 on which every method completed its full run of repeats. This common-denominator rule keeps the comparison fair: no method can inflate its score by silently skipping datasets it cannot complete. We use this same set of 105 throughout; the per-dataset counts in Table IV and elsewhere all share this denominator. The 105 datasets cover software configuration (X264, BDBC, P OSTGRE SQL, LLVM, S TORM , and others), software process modeling (POM3, a model of agile requirements prioritization [45]; XOMO, a Monte Carlo simulator over the COCOMO suite of effort, risk, and defect models [46]), feature models, project health, and hyperparameter optimization.

C. Cost Metrics 

For each (method, dataset, seed) triple, we measure five cost metrics: labels used, LLM tokens (input plus output), wall-clock seconds, number of LLM calls, and dollar cost in our setup. 

D. Statistical Analysis 

We ran twenty seeds per (method, dataset) pair. On each dataset, methods are sorted by median score; the leader (best median) plus all subsequent methods indistinguishable from it form the top tier (stopping at the first method that differs). Membership in this top tier is therefore a per-dataset significance decision, not a raw score comparison: a dataset may place several methods in its top tier, all credited equally. The top-tier rate we report (Table IV) thus aggregates 105 such per-dataset significance tests. To avoid significant-but-meaningless splits [48], two result sets X, Y (sizes n, m ) are judged indistinguishable if both of the following hold:  

> •

Cliff’s delta [49], |δ| = |#( x>y ) − #( x<y )| / (nm ), is below 0.195 (small [50]); and  

> •

the Kolmogorov-Smirnov statistic [51] (max distance between the empirical cumulative distributions) is below 

1.36 p(n + m)/(nm ) (95% confidence). These two standard, distribution-free tests (a small effect size and no significant difference) together decide whether two result sets differ.

VI. D ISCUSSION 

A. Threats to Validity a) Dataset Selection: : are the datasets a representative sample of all SE? Every empirical study faces this threat to validity. The best we can do is make our case study library as large as possible, which is why we used MOOT. The 105 datasets used here are the subset of MOOT’s 127. The excluded datasets are method timeouts, not low-quality data. A method that fails more often is therefore filtered out silently. The datasets are also all tabular. Whether the conclusions transfer to non-tabular SE optimization (test generation, patch synthesis, code optimization) is an open problem. 

b) Construct Validity: : do our evaluations measure what we think they measure? 

∆ is normalized against the empirical minimum and median 

d2h over the dataset rows, not against an external ground truth. Here “optimal” means reference optimal , a term from the empirical-algorithms literature [41], [54]: the best solution observed so far. The reference optimal may not be the true optimal. But in many engineering cases the true optimal is unknown. Because it is only the best observed row, a method’s held-out score can exceed it. This is why ∆ can pass 100% ,as SNAP 2 does on POM 3A (100 .9% ). We also computed hypervolume and Chebyshev distance, and the ranking is consistent. 

c) Shared Machinery: : does SNAP 2 win only because it reuses a baseline? 

> SNAP

2 uses EZR as its first stage, then is compared against  

> EZR

in the same ranking. The two are not independent, so 

> SNAP

2’s advantage could reflect that shared substrate rather than the classical-then-LLM order. Three points bound this. The split was fixed in advance, not searched for the best result. And SNAP 2 also tops the LLM-first opt2 methods, which share 

> SNAP

’s machinery but reverse the order. So the order, not the shared parts, is what moves the ranking. A third point is decisive. SNAP 2 gives its EZR stage only B=10 labels, half the B=20 that the standalone EZR baseline gets (Table II). Its  

> EZR

stage is therefore a weaker EZR than the baseline, yet 

> SNAP

2 still wins. The gain cannot come from the EZR stage alone. 

d) Sample Validity: : did we test enough of the design space? We explore some algorithmic options, but not all. Some omitted options were already assessed at FSE’26, where EZR 

beat SMAC3, TPE, and the qEHVI/MOEA/D family [28]. The other omissions are the opt2 sub-roles: variation operator [22], [55], surrogate [23], caged sampler [24], [56], operator selec-tion [25], and algorithm designer [26], [57]–[59]. These are excluded by design. We hold the host optimizer fixed (the shared SMO loop at B = 20 ) and vary only the degree of LLM involvement. Warm-start seeding is the one sub-role that swaps cleanly onto that loop. Each other sub-role presupposes a different host: an evolutionary loop, a Bayesian surrogate loop, a partition scheduler, or a program-synthesis loop. Importing them would confound the regime effect with a host-optimizer effect. Several also abandon the fixed-B label axis, which makes them incommensurable in our ranking and cost tables. Testing which scaffold earns its keep is left to future work. 

e) External Validity: : do the results generalize beyond our setting?  

> SNAP

and SNAP 2 propose configurations, then project each onto the nearest measured row. They select from a known table rather than generating unseen configurations. This bounds external validity. Our results speak to choosing among configurations whose objective values are already recorded, not to proposing configurations never evaluated. Extending this approach to generative settings, where the optimum may lie off-table, is future work. 

B. Open Issues and Future Work a) A complexity measure for SE optimization.: The ceiling argument above turns the usual question around. Instead of “which optimizer is best,” ask “which problems are hard.” Most  

> MOOT

tasks yield to B = 20 labels (Section V); a few resist. A predictor of that resistance, computed from cheap surface features of a dataset before any optimizer runs, would let a practitioner budget labels per task. It would also explain why so many methods converge. The landscape-sparsity intuition behind our B = 20 argument (Table III) is the natural starting point. 

b) A conditional cascade.: SNAP 2 runs EZR then SNAP 

on every task. But EZR alone already reaches most of SNAP 2’s quality at near-zero cost (Tables IV and V). The open question is when the SNAP stage is worth running at all. A conditional cascade would run EZR first, then escalate to SNAP only on tasks whose EZR result looks uncertain. Paired with the complexity measure above, this could capture SNAP 2’s quality while paying the LLM cost only where it earns its keep. 

c) Beyond tabular selection.: Our methods snap each proposed candidate to the nearest row in the MOOT table, a configuration whose objective values we have already measured. This snapping means the LLM only has to point toward a good region, not name an exact valid configuration, and it can never return an unmeasured or invalid answer. But it only works because MOOT gives us a finite table of scored configurations to snap onto. Other SE optimization tasks have no such table. Test generation, patch synthesis, and code optimization all search an open space, where no candidate is pre-scored and there is nothing to snap to. Whether our results hold once that safety net is removed, and what should replace it, is the main question this paper leaves open. 

d) Scaling with the model.: Our results come from one open-weights model, one seed family, and B = 20 . As models improve, does SNAP 2’s margin over EZR widen, and does EZR remain the stable free anchor? Half of the LLM tokens are unshown reasoning tokens (Section IV). A study of cheaper, non-reasoning prompting would show how much of the advantage is reasoning and how much is the projection trick. VII. C ONCLUSION 

The literature was too pessimistic about LLMs as optimizers for SE. On 105 SE tasks, the best method is neither a classical learner nor an LLM alone, but the two combined. SNAP 2 seeds a short EZR run, then hands its trajectory to an LLM to finish. It reaches the top tier on 85% of tasks, above the same LLM run alone (75%) and above EZR alone (71%). The order of that combination is what wins. Prior work runs the LLM first and lets a classical optimizer finish; those methods top out at 74%. SNAP 2 reverses the order, seeds with the cheap classical learner, and lets the LLM close. Same ingredients, opposite order, and an eleven-point gain (to 85%). The result also costs less. SNAP 2 dominates the LLM-alone method on every axis: higher quality, about 30% fewer tokens, and 1.4× faster, since the classical step does the cheap early work. To conclude, we offer the following guidance to practitioners. When optimizing some SE process:  

> •

First try a classical method (i.e. no LLM). Why? Well: 

– Some of those classical methods, like EZR , are virtually free and perform close to the best known results (yet run three orders of magnitude faster). 

– By first running a very fast classical optimizer, practitioners can get quick feedback. 

– Such classical methods offer a baseline result against which the value of supposedly more valuable systems can be compared. 

– Further, you will need the classical optimizer for the next step.  

> •

Second, try an LLM-based optimizer when developing (a) mission-critical applications or (b) safety-critical applications, or (c) when execution cost is not a concern.
